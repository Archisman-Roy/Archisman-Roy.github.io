Producer Surplus

The mirror image of consumer surplus. Same triangle, flipped upside-down. We'll start with ten freelance developers, build up to a supply curve, and end by laying supply and demand on the same axes — where the market's total gains from trade live.

Part 1The supply problem

You need a SaaS MVP built. Ten freelance developers responded to your posting, and each has a different willingness to accept (WTA) — the minimum monthly rate they'd take before walking away. A principal engineer moonlighting has a high opportunity cost; a bootcamp grad looking for a portfolio piece will take almost anything.

Here's the cast, ranked from lowest WTA to highest. The supply side is conventionally drawn this way — cheapest units first — because that's the order in which sellers enter the market as price rises.

Part 2Set the market price

Pick a single price that everyone is offered. Developers whose WTA sits below the price line supply; those above it stay home — the money's not worth their time. For every seller who supplies, the bar itself is their cost (what they needed to make the trade worth it), and the slice above the bar up to the price line is their producer surplus — the extra they pocket over their reservation.

Drag the slider. Lower prices leave cheap developers with big margins but shut out the high-cost ones. Higher prices pull more developers into the market but shrink everyone's PS per trade — wait, actually the opposite: higher prices grow PS per seller because the gap above their cost widens. (This is the first clue that producer surplus behaves like a mirror image of consumer surplus.)

Pricing strategy

Now flip to Reverse auction. Instead of one price for everyone, each developer is paid exactly their own WTA — imagine a procurement portal where the buyer sees every bid and pays only what each seller demanded. Every bar becomes pure cost — no teal slice above, because no seller got a margin. The buyer captured all the gains from trade. This is the mirror of personalized pricing from the consumer surplus page: whichever side runs the auction keeps the surplus.

Part 3From people to a curve

In a real labor market you'd have thousands of potential sellers, and the ten-bar staircase smooths into an upward-sloping line — the supply curve. It's just sellers lined up by WTA from lowest to highest. The first unit produced is cheap; each additional unit pulls in a more expensive seller, so the curve rises.

Flip between views. In the continuous uniform view, producer surplus is the triangle above the supply curve and below the price line. For a linear supply curve it's ½ × base × height, the exact mirror of the consumer surplus triangle on the other page.

The pricing strategy toggle from Part 2 applies here too. Under a reverse auction the buyer pays each seller's own cost, so the entire area under the supply curve becomes cost paid out and producer surplus collapses to zero. The seller-side equivalent of "you captured the whole rectangle" is "they captured none of it."

Part 4Supply meets demand

Now the payoff. A real market has both sides: the ten developers from this page on the supply side, and the ten buyers from the consumer surplus page on the demand side. Stack them on the same axes, smooth into continuous curves, and you get the picture every intro textbook leads with: two lines that cross at equilibrium.

Three regions live between them. The green triangle above the price line is consumer surplus. The teal triangle below it is producer surplus. Together they are total surplus — the gains from trade, the reason the market exists at all.

Drag the slider. At the equilibrium price $25 the two curves cross and every beneficial trade happens: total surplus is maximized at $125. Push the price above equilibrium and demand collapses to the short side — sellers would supply more, but no buyers left to buy from. Push it below and supply collapses instead. Either way, a red triangle of deadweight loss opens up: trades that would have benefited both parties, strangled by a price set at the wrong level.

Part 5Why this matters in tech

Once you see the symmetry, a lot of product and platform decisions snap into focus. Here are three cases where producer surplus is the interesting side of the chart.

Uber surge pricing — moving up the supply curve

Without surge, the price is a ceiling below the true equilibrium on busy nights: demand spikes, supply stays flat at the regulated rate, and the market clears with a shortage. Surge lifts the price until more drivers log on — literally moving up the supply curve. Riders grumble about the split (PS grows, CS shrinks), but the quantity traded rises, and the deadweight-loss triangle of "people waiting 40 minutes" collapses.

Cloud spot instances — selling idle capacity

The supply curve for spot compute is nearly flat near zero: the servers exist whether they're used or not, so the marginal cost of a spot-hour is essentially zero. Any price above that is pure producer surplus. AWS and GCP's spot markets exist precisely because the alternative — idling the hardware — is a pile of unrealized PS that someone out there would happily pay for at any price.

App Store 70/30 — splitting the surplus

When Apple takes 30% of app revenue, it's negotiating the split of a specific total-surplus rectangle. Apple argues the 30% is payment for distribution, trust, and a billion-device install base — services that grow the total pie by bringing buyers and sellers together who otherwise wouldn't meet. Developers argue the 30% is a tax that transfers PS to the platform without growing the pie at all. Both arguments live or die on whether the platform actually expands the market.

Part 6Surge pricing up close

It's a Friday night. A concert lets out, it starts raining, the train line is down. Suddenly the number of people who want a ride has doubled, and they are willing to pay more to get home. In supply-demand language, the demand curve has shifted outward — at every price, the quantity demanded is higher. The supply curve hasn't moved: there's a fixed pool of drivers on the road right now.

Drag the demand shift slider to move from a normal evening (0) to a full New Year's Eve spike (30). Then flip the surge toggle. With surge off, price is pinned at the normal-night equilibrium of $25 — effectively a price ceiling. With surge on, price floats up to whatever clears the new market, drawing more drivers onto the road and rationing rides to the people who value them most.

Dial the demand shift all the way to 30 with surge off. Only five rides complete — the supply curve literally can't produce more at $25 — while nearly ten riders are left at the curb. The red triangle is the deadweight loss, and it's made of trades that both sides wanted to happen but a capped price prevented.

Flip surge on. Price jumps, but look at the chart: the deadweight-loss triangle vanishes. More drivers log on (the quantity traded grows along the supply curve), and the unserved-demand bar disappears. Total surplus is maximized — the exact same principle as Part 4, just with a demand curve that moves.

The redistribution is real, though. A rider who was willing to pay $25 on a normal night now pays the surge price instead, and that extra flows to the driver. Surge grows the pie and hands a larger share to the supply side. The policy debate around it is rarely about efficiency — it's almost always about whether that redistribution is acceptable in the rain.
