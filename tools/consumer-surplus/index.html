Consumer Surplus

An interactive walkthrough of demand curves, consumer surplus, and why uniform pricing always leaves value on the table. We'll start with ten fictional users and build our way up to the triangle behind every pricing page you've ever seen.

Part 1The pricing problem

You're launching a SaaS productivity app. Ten people are interested, and each has a different willingness to pay (WTP) — the maximum monthly price they'd accept before walking away. A startup founder who saves ten hours a week values the tool very differently from a casual user who opens it twice a month.

Part 2Set your price

Pick a single price for every user. The dashed orange line is your price. Users whose bar clears it buy; the rest walk away. For every buyer, the slice of their bar above the line is the deal they got — their consumer surplus. The rectangle below the line, spanning the buyers, is your revenue.

The gray bars on the right are users who valued the app but were priced out. Economists call that missed value deadweight loss — nobody captures it. Drag the slider to feel the tradeoff: lower prices grow surplus but shrink revenue per buyer; higher prices do the opposite and strand more users.

Pricing strategy

Now flip to Personalized. Instead of one price for everyone, each buyer pays exactly their own WTP. Every bar becomes pure revenue — no green slice above the line, because no buyer got a deal. Nobody is priced out, so deadweight loss disappears too. Revenue jumps from the single rectangle to the full area under the staircase: $45 + $38 + … + $3 = $215. This is the upper bound of what any pricing scheme can extract, and the reason companies work so hard to approximate it with tiers, usage metering, and auctions.

Part 3From people to a curve

Ten bars is a toy example. In a real market you'd have thousands of potential buyers with slightly different WTPs, and the staircase blurs into a smooth downward-sloping line — the demand curve. It's just the lineup of all buyers, ranked from highest WTP to lowest.

Flip between the two views. In the continuous uniform view, consumer surplus is the triangle above the price line, revenue is the rectangle below, and deadweight loss is the triangle to the right of the equilibrium quantity. For a linear demand curve, each of these is just ½ × base × height.

The pricing strategy toggle from Part 2 applies here too. Personalized pricing floods the whole area under the demand curve with revenue: the triangle has area ½ × 10 × $50 = $250. That's the theoretical ceiling — surplus and deadweight loss both drop to zero.

Part 4Why this matters in tech

Every pricing strategy in tech is, at its core, an attempt to capture more of the area under the demand curve. A single uniform price leaves two triangles on the table: the surplus the lucky high-WTP users pocketed, and the deadweight loss from everyone priced out. Here are three ways real products try to reshape that rectangle.

SaaS tiers — Slack, Notion, Figma

A Free tier captures the long tail that would otherwise be deadweight loss — users who'd pay $0 but not $10, who now generate network effects, funnel prospects, and ad impressions. A Pro tier sits near the middle of the demand curve and a per-seat Enterprise tier extracts surplus from high-WTP organizations. Three horizontal slices do a better job of covering the area under the curve than any single price.

App Store pricing — $0.99 vs. $4.99 vs. free-with-ads

At $4.99 you earn more per buyer but lose the casual downloaders. At $0.99 you flood the market and leave surplus on the table with power users. Free-with-ads dodges the choice entirely: heavy users see more ads and generate more revenue, so price scales with value without ever asking the user for a number.

Cloud spot pricing — AWS, GCP

Spot instances let buyers bid their WTP for interruptible compute. High-value, latency-sensitive workloads pay on-demand rates; fault-tolerant batch jobs pay a fraction. The clearing price floats to meet supply, which means far less of the demand curve is left unsold than a fixed-price menu would leave.

Consumer surplus isn't just a textbook concept. It's the invisible force behind every pricing page, every free tier, every enterprise sales call, and every auction on the internet.
