The vector $x = (1,2,3)$ lives in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — one axis per observation. The column space $\text{Col}(X)$ is the line through the origin along $x$: every possible fitted vector $\hat{y} = \beta \cdot x$ lies somewhere on this line. OLS finds the point on this line closest to $y$, which is the orthogonal projection. The residual $e$ is perpendicular to the line — that's the normal equation $x'e = 0$ in action.

Section 2: Two predictors, a plane

With one predictor, $\text{Col}(X)$ was a line. Add a second predictor and it becomes a plane — a 2D subspace in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The projection $\hat{y}$ is now the closest point on this plane to $y$, and the residual $e$ is perpendicular to the entire plane.

The normal equations $X'e = 0$ say that $e$ is perpendicular to every column of $X$ — both $x_1$ and $x_2$. Since these columns span the plane, $e$ is perpendicular to the entire plane. This is the defining property of orthogonal projection, and it's exactly what 'least squares' means geometrically: the shortest path from $y$ to a subspace is always perpendicular.

Key Takeaways

  1. $\hat{y}$ is a projection: $\hat{y} = Hy$ is the orthogonal projection of $y$ onto $\text{Col}(X)$.
  2. $e$ is orthogonal: The residual $e = y - \hat{y}$ satisfies $X'e = 0$, meaning $e \perp \text{Col}(X)$.
  3. Pythagoras gives the ANOVA decomposition: $\|y\|^2 = \|\hat{y}\|^2 + \|e\|^2$, i.e., $SST = SSR + SSE$.
  4. Adding predictors expands the subspace: More columns in $X$ → larger $\text{Col}(X)$ → projection can only get closer to $y$ → $R^2$ can only increase.
  5. $\text{tr}(H) = p$ gives degrees of freedom: The model "uses up" $p$ degrees of freedom; the residual has $n - p$.
