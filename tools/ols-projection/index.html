OLS as Projection

Ordinary Least Squares finds the closest point in the column space of $X$ to the response vector $y$. This is an orthogonal projection in observation space ($\mathbb{R}^n$). Drag the 3D plots to rotate.

One Predictor: Projection onto a Line

With a single predictor, the column space $\text{Col}(X)$ is a line through the origin. The fitted vector $\hat{y} = \hat{\beta} x$ is the point on this line closest to $y$.

$x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$
$y = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$
Both vectors live in $\mathbb{R}^n$ where $n = 3$ is the number of observations. Each coordinate axis represents one observation — not a variable.
Why are the axes observations?

The usual regression plot flips this view. Seeing the dual representation is what lets us talk about OLS as a projection.

Scatterplot view
The familiar one
  • Axes = variables (predictor, response)
  • Each point = one observation
  • $n$ observations → $n$ points in a 2D plot
Observation space
This view
  • Axes = observations (obs 1, obs 2, obs 3)
  • Each vector = one variable — $x$ and $y$ each collapse to a single arrow
  • $n$ observations → vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, regardless of how many variables you have

Why bother with the flip? Because $y$ and every column of $X$ are $n$-dimensional vectors in the same space, you can ask: "What is the closest point in $\text{Col}(X)$ to $y$?" That's a projection — a geometric question with a geometric answer. In the scatterplot view the question is not even well-posed.

Concrete bridge. With your current inputs $x =$ ... and $y =$ ...:

  • Scatterplot view: three points — ...
  • Observation-space view: two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — one pointing to ..., the other to ...

Same data, two representations.

Scaling. A real dataset with $n = 100$ observations lives in $\mathbb{R}^{100}$. The geometry is identical to what's shown here — we use $n = 3$ only so it fits on a page. Adding predictors doesn't enlarge the ambient space; it adds more vectors whose span becomes $\text{Col}(X)$.

The residual $e$ is perpendicular to $x$ — that's the normal equation $x'e = 0$. The Pythagorean theorem $\|y\|^2 = \|\hat{y}\|^2 + \|e\|^2$ confirms the right angle: this is the ANOVA decomposition $SST = SSR + SSE$.

Two Predictors: Projection onto a Plane

Adding a second predictor expands the column space from a line to a 2D plane. The projection $\hat{y} = Hy$ is the closest point on this plane to $y$, where $H = X(X'X)^{-1}X'$ is the hat matrix.

$X = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$
$y = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$
$X$ is $n \times p$ with $n = 3$ observations and $p = 2$ predictors. Each column of $X$ is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Together they span a 2D plane — the column space $\text{Col}(X)$.

The trace equals the number of predictors — this is where "degrees of freedom consumed by the model" comes from.

The normal equations $X'e = 0$ say that $e$ is perpendicular to every column of $X$ — both $x_1$ and $x_2$. Since these columns span the plane, $e \perp \text{Col}(X)$. This is the defining property of orthogonal projection: the shortest path from $y$ to a subspace is always perpendicular.

Key Takeaways

  1. $\hat{y}$ is a projection: $\hat{y} = Hy$ is the orthogonal projection of $y$ onto $\text{Col}(X)$.
  2. $e$ is orthogonal: The residual $e = y - \hat{y}$ satisfies $X'e = 0$, meaning $e \perp \text{Col}(X)$.
  3. Pythagoras gives the ANOVA decomposition: $\|y\|^2 = \|\hat{y}\|^2 + \|e\|^2$, i.e., $SST = SSR + SSE$.
  4. Adding predictors expands the subspace: More columns in $X$ → larger $\text{Col}(X)$ → projection can only get closer to $y$ → $R^2$ can only increase.
  5. $\text{tr}(H) = p$ gives degrees of freedom: The model uses $p$ degrees of freedom; the residual has $n - p$.
